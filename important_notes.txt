## Papers relevant to your split strategy

### 1. AirfRANS (NeurIPS 2022) — the most directly relevant

This is the benchmark dataset most comparable to your situation. It proposes four distinct ML tasks with different generalization constraints: full data regime, scarce data regime, Reynolds number extrapolation, and angle of attack extrapolation.

Concretely, the full data regime uses 800 simulations for training and 200 for testing, both drawn from the same distribution — defining an interpolation task. The scarce data regime uses the same test set but only 200 simulations for training. The Reynolds extrapolation regime keeps simulations with Reynolds numbers between 3 and 5 million for training, and the others for testing. The angle of attack extrapolation regime keeps simulations with AoA between −2.5° and 12.5° for training, and the others for testing.

And importantly, extrapolation tasks are confirmed to be more difficult than interpolation tasks, with Spearman correlation for the lift coefficient being significantly lower for both extrapolation tasks than for the interpolation ones.

**→ Direct application to your challenge:** this gives you a clean 2-phase structure: Phase 1 = interpolation (random AoA split), Phase 2 = extrapolation (entire Mach or pi level excluded).

---

### 2. NeurIPS 2024 ML4CFD Competition — explicit in/out-of-distribution split

The competition used a training set from a scarce regime filtered to Reynolds numbers between 3×10⁶ and 5×10⁶, an in-distribution test set of 200 samples within the same Reynolds regime, and an out-of-distribution test set of 496 samples with extrapolated Reynolds ranges outside the training.

**→ This is exactly a two-phase structure:** Phase 1 tests interpolation, Phase 2 tests extrapolation on Reynolds — directly applicable to your pi split idea.

---

### 3. Neural Fields / MARIO paper — boundary of parameter space is hardest

The highest errors were observed at the boundaries of the parameter space, particularly when extrapolating to combinations of parameters not well-represented in the training data.

**→ Implication for your split:** putting extreme Mach or extreme AoA in the test set for Phase 2 is physically well-motivated, not arbitrary.

---

## Why kNN works surprisingly well — the manifold argument

This is a deep and interesting question. Several papers point to the same explanation.

### The core reason: aerodynamic fields live on a very low-dimensional manifold

Research has shown that nonlinear vortical flow fields over time and parameter space can be compressed to only three variables using a lift-augmented autoencoder, while retaining the essence of the original high-dimensional physics. This reveals that the fundamental physics behind aerodynamics is far simpler and lower-rank than traditionally expected.

In the ONERA paper specifically, the IsoMap method confirms this: the latent space dimension is found to be r=3, **exactly matching the 3 physical input parameters** (M∞, AoA, pi). This means the manifold of flow fields is intrinsically 3-dimensional.

If the manifold is 3D and well-sampled, then kNN in that 3D space is essentially **exact local interpolation** — no learning is needed, just weighted averaging of nearby snapshots.

### Why it fails on wrMAE despite good R²

kNN breaks down precisely when:
- The local flow **topology changes discontinuously** (e.g. shock appears, flow separates) between two neighbors
- The test point falls near a **boundary of the parameter space** where neighbors are only on one side
- The mapping from conditions to fields is **locally nonlinear** — which is exactly what happens at high AoA and high M∞

A key challenge in data-centric approaches is that rare events such as high or low velocities are not adequately represented in the database, and kNN-based methods struggle to extrapolate to conditions far beyond available training data.

### Summary table

| Why kNN works well | Why kNN fails |
|---|---|
| Fields lie on a ~3D manifold | Topology changes discontinuously near shocks |
| Dense sampling → neighbors are very close | Boundary of parameter space → asymmetric neighbors |
| Linear interpolation is locally valid for smooth regimes | High AoA/transonic → locally nonlinear |
| No overfitting risk, no hyperparameter sensitivity | wrMAE captures exactly these failure cases |

---

## Bottom line for your challenge design

The AirfRANS 4-task structure is your best reference. For density ρ specifically, you gain an extra dimension: the **volumetric field** means you can add a **spatial extrapolation** task (train on far-field, test on near-wall) that has no equivalent in the ONERA surface-only challenge — and that's where kNN would collapse completely, since spatial proximity in the field has no direct relation to proximity in the (M∞, AoA, pi) parameter space.
